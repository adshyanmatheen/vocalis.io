<h4 align="center">Introduction</h4>
<br>
<p align="justify">The common challenges English learners face are pronunciation difficulties from first-language phonetic interference (L1) and the scarcity of digital tools for personalized feedback.</p>

<p align="justify">Pronunciation difficulties often stem from a learner's first language and limited exposure to native speech patterns. Korean speakers, whose L1 restricts coda position to seven unreleased stops, may pronounce "park" as "pakeu." Mandarin speakers replace "th" with "s," pronouncing "think" as "sink." Tamil and Sinhala speakers, whose L1s lack phonemic contrast for several English fricatives, may pronounce "fan" as "pan" or "van" as "ban," and "street" as "sitreet." Although minor, these deviations hinder listener comprehension and reduce speaker confidence.</p>

<p align="justify">Interlanguage refers to the transitional linguistic system that develops during second language learning, consisting of L1 features mixed with target language features (Selinker, 1972; Tarone, 2006). Learner errors are not random but systematic patterns reflecting the learning process itself (Corder, 1967). However, interlanguage theory is often overlooked by CAPT systems, which treat errors as deviations from a fixed norm rather than artifacts of an evolving linguistic system.</p>

<p align="justify">To overcome this problem, Computer-Assisted Pronunciation Training (CAPT) provides scalable real-time feedback (Amrate & Tsai, 2024). However, most systems rely on static acoustic models and a one-size-fits-all approach. They offer scores or "correct / incorrect" feedback without explaining why errors occur or how to correct them (El Kheir et al., 2023). Recent research suggests LLMs may bridge this gap through more adaptive, transparent, and learner-centred feedback.</p>

<p>Pronunciation feedback falls into three levels: Knowledge of Results, Knowledge of Performance, and Instructional Feedback (Darcy et al., 2021). Knowledge of Results indicates whether pronunciation was correct or incorrect. Knowledge of Performance shows deviation through visual-acoustic displays. Instructional feedback provides articulatory guidance on what went wrong and how to fix it. Existing CAPT systems operate only at the first two levels.</p>

<p>Consequently, existing systems emphasize accuracy without meaningful guidance. Improvements in detection do not automatically translate into learning support (El Kheir et al., 2023). At the same time, these systems provide generic feedback regardless of individual pronunciation patterns, offering broadly similar feedback to all users (Amrate & Tsai, 2024). The core problem is the absence of a framework for personalized, explainable, and interlanguage-aware feedback.</p>
